## III. Why the April 16 Admission Exposes Fraud on the Court

### A. Destruction of "Good Faith" (Scienter)

In any fraud action, scienter—the conscious intent to deceive—is the hardest element to prove. Defendants routinely assert that a false statement was a "good faith mistake," a "misunderstanding," or a "clerical error."

The April 16 admission annihilates that defense. By explicitly naming "Dolan" as the source of the body cams, Navaratnasingham demonstrates **affirmative, specific, and historical knowledge** of the evidence's chain of custody. One cannot casually name the specific law firm that handed over a piece of evidence years earlier if one is genuinely ignorant of that evidence's existence. The dismissive phrasing—"for whatever, sure"—reveals that possession of these records was a **recognized, established fact** within the defense trial team.

Therefore, when she previously stated that the defense "did not receive the 911 call until the day of the MSC" and that it was "news to me," it was not a mistake about what was in the file. It was a calculated linguistic maneuver designed to exploit the judge's focus on subpoena returns and timeliness, deliberately suppressing the defense's actual knowledge that the materials had been provided by Dolan during discovery.

### B. Judicial Estoppel

California courts enforce the doctrine of **Judicial Estoppel**, which prevents a party from asserting a factual position in a legal proceeding that is contrary to a position they successfully urged earlier in the same proceeding.

**Position A (The Suppression):** "Our office did not receive the 911 call until the day of the MSC." "This is news to me." "We did not have those two items until the day of the MSC." "This is not anything that we received as part of the subpoena." These positions were urged to block admission of evidence, deny continuances, and exclude the supplemental report.

**Position B (The Admission):** "Sure. They were provided by Dolan. He has had them for whatever, sure." This position was taken when it became tactically convenient to acknowledge the exhibit list and discuss the defense's curated body cam presentation.

A party cannot have it both ways. By capturing both statements on the official reporter's transcript, Plaintiff has closed-circuit proof of judicial estoppel. The Court cannot uphold a verdict where the prevailing party successfully utilized two mutually exclusive factual realities to manipulate the trial's evidentiary rulings.

### C. The Statutory Breach: Candor and Artifice

**California Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 3.3 (Candor Toward the Tribunal):** An attorney shall not knowingly make a false statement of fact or law to a tribunal. Rule 3.3(a)(1) also mandates that an attorney must **correct** a false statement of material fact previously made. By making the "subpoena" and "news to me" statements to block evidence, and later making the "Dolan" statement without correcting the former, Navaratnasingham engaged in an ongoing violation.

**Business and Professions Code § 6068(d):** An attorney must employ "those means only as are consistent with truth, and never to seek to mislead the judge or any judicial officer by an **artifice** or false statement of fact or law."

The "subpoena shield" was an artifice—a legally crafted illusion. By answering a question about possession with a statement about subpoena returns, counsel manufactured a factual void. Discovering this artifice retroactively is the ultimate professional betrayal: the Court's authority was weaponized to suppress the truth.

### D. Extrinsic Fraud (Fraus Illata Curiae)

**Intrinsic Fraud:** A witness lies on the stand about a fact (e.g., the color of a traffic light). The adversary system expects opposing counsel to expose this through cross-examination. The system is working as designed.

**Extrinsic Fraud:** Opposing counsel lies **directly to the judge** about possessing the body cam footage of the traffic light, causing the judge to exclude it from the trial entirely. The latter is an existential threat to the adversary system.

Here, the defense did not present false evidence for the jury to weigh. They erected a **collateral barrier** that prevented Plaintiff from fully exhibiting his case. By keeping Plaintiff and the Court "in ignorance" of the true evidentiary record via an affirmative artifice (B&P Code § 6068(d)), the defense committed extrinsic fraud. This grants the equity court the inherent power to vacate the judgment entirely under *Kulchar v. Kulchar* (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467 and *Aldrich v. San Francisco Superior Court* (1983) 151 Cal.App.3d 720.
