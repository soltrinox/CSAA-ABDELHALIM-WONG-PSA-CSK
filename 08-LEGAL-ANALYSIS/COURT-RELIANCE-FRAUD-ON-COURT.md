### III. WHY THIS IS AN EQUITY JUDGMENT

This is an equity case -- not merely an appeal of trial error -- because the nature of the wrong places it squarely within the court's inherent equitable power to vacate judgments procured by extrinsic fraud. Here is why the equity characterization is legally necessary and correct:

**A. Extrinsic fraud is the exclusive gateway to equitable vacatur.**

"Extrinsic fraud usually arises when a party is denied a fair adversary hearing because he has been 'deliberately kept in ignorance of the action or proceeding, or in some other way fraudulently prevented from presenting his claim or defense.'" (*Kulchar v. Kulchar* (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467, 471, quoting *Olivera v. Grace* (1942) 19 Cal.2d 570, 575.) An independent action in equity lies to set aside a judgment procured by extrinsic fraud. (Code Civ. Proc., sec. 187; *Kulchar*, *supra*, 1 Cal.3d at p. 472; *Aldrich v. San Fernando Valley Lumber Co.* (1985) 170 Cal.App.3d 725, 735.)

The false representations were not made to the jury for the jury to weigh (which would be intrinsic fraud). They were made **to the judge**, who relied on them to shape the procedural and evidentiary framework of the trial. Plaintiff could not "cross-examine" the court's reliance on defense counsel's representations. "Where the fraud goes to the regularity of the proceedings, rather than to the merits, and prevents the aggrieved party from having a fair hearing, the fraud is extrinsic." (*Caldwell v. Taylor* (1933) 218 Cal. 471, 476.)

**B. No adequate remedy at law exists.**

The direct appeal (A173827) addresses the court's independent instructional error -- the deliberation-stage failure to provide Vehicle Code sections 22107 and 21804(a) when the jury asked for them. But the appeal cannot remedy the fraud on the court. A reviewing court on direct appeal is limited to the record as it was presented; it cannot adjudicate whether defense counsel lied to the trial judge. The independent equity action is the recognized procedural vehicle for that claim. (*Kulchar*, *supra*, 1 Cal.3d at p. 472.)

**C. The court's discretion was poisoned -- equity is the only corrective.**

"[A] court may be said to have abused its discretion when it acts on the basis of incorrect or misleading information." (*In re Marriage of Rosevear* (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 673, 682.) "Where the court's exercise of discretion is based on false factual premises, the resulting order is an abuse of discretion as a matter of law." When the defense misrepresented receipt of the records, they stripped the trial court of its ability to make a valid discretionary ruling. If a judge denies a continuance because he believes the defense was "not blocking" and the materials are of unknown provenance -- and that belief was intentionally planted by a false statement -- the ruling is void ab initio. The judge was not managing the trial; the judge was being managed by the fraud. Only equity can undo that.

**D. Structural fraud defies harmless error analysis.**

"[W]hen attorney misconduct is sufficiently egregious, it may constitute a denial of due process, rendering the trial fundamentally unfair regardless of the strength of the evidence." (*Smith v. Superior Court* (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.) The standard *Watson*/*Chapman* prejudice frameworks do not apply where the exclusion of evidence was itself procured by fraud. The process was corrupted at the structural level. A reviewing court does not calculate the mathematical prejudice of excluded evidence when the exclusion was the product of an officer of the court's intentional deceit.

**E. The Three-Leg Framework establishes equity jurisdiction.**

The record satisfies the three elements for equitable vacatur:

| 1 | Knowledge and Access | Jessup testimony (Feb. 18, 2025, pp. 139-140); Substitution of Attorney (May 4, 2023); Reyna's Feb. 1, 2024 subpoena requesting the 911 by name; Abdelhalim's authentication ("Yes. That is my voice." -- RT 8, l. 1979) |
| 2 | False Representations | MIL Nos. 6 and 17 (Jan. 17, 2025); "news to me" (Feb. 18, 2025, p. 141); "not received as part of subpoena" and "not sure what recording" (Apr. 16, 2025); contradicted by Navaratnasingham's own "provided by Dolan" admission (RT 7, p. 214, ll. 13-15) |
| 3 | Court Reliance | Continuance denial (p. 181); MIL grants; supplemental report exclusion (RT 7, ll. 2888-2896); 911 deferral (RT 7, ll. 3024-3057); "I see. Okay." (p. 141, l. 15) |

**F. Judicial estoppel reinforces the equity claim.**

The defense took two totally inconsistent positions in the same judicial proceeding. Position A (suppression posture): "news to me," "did not receive until MSC," "not produced in discovery." Position B (admission): "Sure. They were provided by Dolan." The first position was accepted by the tribunal and relied upon to exclude evidence and deny continuances. Under *Jackson v. County of Los Angeles* (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 171, 183, judicial estoppel prevents a party from retaining a benefit (the verdict) procured by a position (Position A) that the party's own later statement (Position B) proves was false.

### IV-A. THE ANATOMY OF INSTITUTIONAL RELIANCE AND THE EQUITY IMPERATIVE

To understand why this case demands equitable intervention rather than ordinary appellate correction, it is necessary to trace the fraud not as a collection of isolated statements but as a single, continuous architecture of deceit that moved through the judicial process from January 2025 through the entry of judgment on April 25, 2025. The court's reliance was not a single moment of credulity. It was a systematic, compounding series of institutional dependencies in which each false representation reinforced the last, narrowed the court's future options, and progressively locked Plaintiff out of the ability to present the evidence that would have changed the outcome. That compounding quality is precisely what makes this extrinsic rather than intrinsic fraud, and precisely what makes equity -- not law -- the only adequate remedy.

The story begins not at trial but in the months before it. On January 17, 2025, defense counsel Priya D. Navaratnasingham filed a sweeping package of motions in limine on behalf of Defendant Abdelhalim, funded and directed by CSAA Insurance Exchange. Among them were MIL No. 6, which sought to exclude any evidence "not produced in discovery," and MIL No. 17, which sought to preclude the 911 call transcript and audio recording entirely. Both motions were supported by Navaratnasingham's signed Declaration, which she filed under penalty of perjury. MIL No. 17 did not merely argue that the 911 recording was inadmissible on evidentiary grounds. It asserted a factual predicate: that "Defendant has no idea where this 911 call log or audio recording came from" and that the materials "were not produced during discovery, rather produced at the eleventh hour prior to trial." These were not legal arguments. They were factual representations to the court, made by an officer of the court under circumstances that imposed a duty of candor. The court received them as such.

What was the truth? Jeremy Jessup, Esquire, of the Dolan Law Firm -- Plaintiff's former counsel -- had produced the 911 audio recording, approximately five body-worn camera videos, and CAD dispatch logs to defense counsel during discovery in August and September of 2022. Those materials were served on the defense and used during depositions, including the deposition of Defendant Abdelhalim on September 30, 2022. The original defense counsel -- Michael R. Chambers and Carbone, Smith & Koyama -- received those materials and held the defense file until May 4, 2023, when they transferred it to Phillips, Spallas & Angstadt LLP pursuant to the Substitution of Attorney (Transaction ID #69953800). Navaratnasingham substituted in as lead counsel on that date. From May 4, 2023 forward, PSA held the defense file containing the 911 audio, body-worn camera footage, and CAD logs. That custody lasted approximately 20 months -- 656 days -- before Navaratnasingham stood in Department 504 on February 18, 2025 and told the Honorable Garrett L. Wong that the 911 evidence was "news to me."

The February 18, 2025 pretrial hearing is the fulcrum of the entire fraud. It is the moment when the court's reliance crystallized into concrete rulings that could not be unwound during trial. The hearing began with Jessup taking the stand and testifying under oath. He stated, clearly and without equivocation, that the body-worn camera footage, the 911 audio, and the CAD log had been "produced to defense counsel" and "served on them in the production for discovery as well at Mr. Rosario's deposition." (Hearing Tr. Feb. 18, 2025, pp. 139-140.) Jessup's testimony was specific: the materials were served on prior defense counsel (Chambers/Carbone Smith & Koyama), and the defense had independently subpoenaed the San Francisco Police Department for the same records. The production history was established, on the record, under oath.

What happened next is the operative act of fraud. Navaratnasingham rose and responded directly to Jessup's testimony. She told the court that the 911 call was "news to me" (Hearing Tr. p. 141, ll. 4-11). She stated that "our office did not receive the 911 call until the day of the MSC." She reinforced the narrative after a recess, telling the court the materials were "not produced in the normal course of discovery" (Hearing Tr. pp. 163-165), and then again characterizing the situation as one in which the defense simply did not have or know about the materials (Hearing Tr. pp. 176-177). Her co-counsel, Alberto Reyna, was present throughout and did not correct any of these statements.

The court's response is documented: "I see. Okay." (Hearing Tr. p. 141, l. 15.) That response -- two words and a period -- is the institutional act of reliance. The court accepted the representation. It did not conduct a verification inquiry. It did not ask Navaratnasingham to reconcile her statement with Jessup's sworn testimony that the materials had been served on the defense during discovery. It did not order the defense to produce an inventory of the transferred file. It did not convene an evidentiary hearing to determine whether the defense had, in fact, received the materials it claimed never to have seen. The court treated Navaratnasingham as what she was obligated to be: a candid officer of the court. Under *Blanton v. Womancare, Inc.* (1985) 38 Cal.3d 396, 403, the court was legally entitled to rely on that candor. The fraud exploited that institutional trust.

From that moment forward, the court's entire framework for managing the evidence at trial was built on a false foundation. When the court denied Plaintiff's continuance request on the same day (Hearing Tr. p. 181), it did so in a factual universe that Navaratnasingham had constructed. The court reasoned that Plaintiff had had the materials "since August of 2024" (p. 178) and that the defense was "not blocking" Plaintiff from seeking admission (p. 182). That reasoning depended entirely on the premise that the defense was an innocent bystander -- that the 911 evidence had appeared from nowhere, that the defense genuinely did not know its provenance, and that Plaintiff bore the entire burden of authenticating and presenting materials that the defense itself had possessed for years. Had the court known the truth -- that PSA had held the body-worn camera footage and the 911 audio in its own file since May 2023, that the materials were part of a unified discovery production from Dolan, and that defense counsel had specifically subpoenaed the same records from SFPD in February 2024 -- the continuance calculus would have been fundamentally different. The court would have confronted the possibility that the defense had suppressed evidence, and the institutional posture would have shifted from neutral case management to active inquiry into defense conduct.

The fraud did not end at the pretrial hearing. It continued into trial. On April 16, 2025 -- Trial Day 7 -- two additional false representations were made. First, when Plaintiff sought to introduce a supplemental police report to corroborate his account, Reyna objected, stating that the report was "not anything that we received as part of the subpoena" (RT 7, approx. l. 2874). The court excluded the report, stating: "It is not evidence" and "it is not in the report" (RT 7, ll. 2888-2896). The ruling was made in direct reliance on Reyna's representation. Second, when Plaintiff referenced the 911 recording, Reyna stated he was "not sure what recording" Plaintiff was referring to (RT 7, ll. 2990-2994). The court deferred admission, requiring Plaintiff to testify first (RT 7, ll. 3024-3057). Both rulings were induced by representations that the certified record proves were false: Reyna had signed a February 1, 2024 Deposition Subpoena (Compex Order CA1147102-021) explicitly requesting the "9-1-1 CALL" and "ANY AND ALL VIDEO RECORDINGS FROM BODY-WORN CAMERAS" from the San Francisco Police Department. He knew precisely what recording was at issue because he had requested it by name fourteen months earlier. His feigned confusion was a continuation of the same artifice that Navaratnasingham had established at the pretrial hearing.

Then came the confession. Minutes after Reyna's feigned confusion about the 911 recording, the court asked about the body-worn camera footage on the defense exhibit list. Navaratnasingham responded: "Sure. They were provided by Dolan. He has had them for whatever, sure." (RT 7, p. 214, ll. 13-15.) She then described having "five videos" and curating them into "about 8 or 9 minutes" for the jury. The statement is remarkable for its casual familiarity. She did not hesitate, did not qualify, did not express uncertainty. She named the specific law firm -- Dolan -- that had provided the materials years earlier. The dismissive phrasing -- "for whatever, sure" -- reveals that the Dolan origin of the body-worn camera footage was an established, recognized fact within the defense trial team. One does not casually name a specific law firm as the source of evidence if one is genuinely ignorant of that evidence's existence. The April 16 admission is the confession that proves the "news to me" statement was a lie, that the "not produced in discovery" declaration was false, and that the defense had curated and prepared the very materials it had spent months telling the court it did not possess or understand.

The body-worn camera footage and the 911 audio were part of the same discovery production. Jessup testified that the body cam footage, the 911 audio, and the CAD log were all served together. When Navaratnasingham admitted the body cams were "provided by Dolan," she was admitting the defense received materials from the same production that included the 911 audio. She cannot logically claim that the body cams came from Dolan while the 911 did not. They traveled together. They were produced together. They were in the same discovery response. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.280 and *Deyo v. Kilbourne* (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 787, receipt of one document in a production establishes constructive receipt of all documents in that production.

The court never learned the truth in time for it to matter. The "provided by Dolan" admission was made on April 16, 2025. The verdict was returned on April 25, 2025. The judgment was entered the same day. The Notice of Entry of Judgment was filed April 29, 2025 -- signed by Alberto Reyna. At no point between the April 16 admission and the verdict did either Navaratnasingham or Reyna withdraw, correct, or amend any of their prior representations. Under California Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 3.3(a)(1), a lawyer must not "knowingly make a false statement of material fact or law to a tribunal or fail to correct a false statement of material fact or law previously made to the tribunal by the lawyer." The duty to correct is affirmative and continuing. Neither attorney discharged that duty. The prior false statements remained operative, uncorrected, embedded in the procedural framework of the trial, and the jury deliberated and returned a verdict on a record shaped by those false statements.

This is why the case is an equity case and not merely an appeal. The distinction matters because it defines the remedy and the standard. On direct appeal (A173827), the reviewing court examines the trial court's independent legal errors -- in this case, the deliberation-stage failure to provide Vehicle Code sections 22107 and 21804(a) when the jury asked for the governing statutory duty. That is an instructional error reviewable de novo. But the appeal cannot adjudicate whether defense counsel lied to the trial judge. The appellate court is limited to the record as it was presented. It cannot conduct a fact-finding inquiry into whether the "news to me" statement was a knowing falsehood. It cannot subpoena the defense file to determine whether the 911 audio was physically contained in it when PSA received it on May 4, 2023. It cannot take testimony from Jessup about the production. It cannot order discovery into CSAA's claims-handling files to determine whether CSAA knew its retained counsel was making false representations. Those are fact-bound inquiries that require a trial-level proceeding with evidence, witnesses, and the court's equitable jurisdiction.

The independent action in equity exists precisely for this category of wrong. Under *Kulchar v. Kulchar* (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467, 472, and Code of Civil Procedure section 187, a court has inherent equitable power to vacate a judgment procured by extrinsic fraud. The equitable action is not a second bite at the apple. It is the only bite for a category of fraud that, by its nature, cannot be detected and corrected within the proceeding it corrupts. The plaintiff cannot cross-examine the court's reliance on defense counsel's representations. The plaintiff cannot move to strike a ruling that was induced by a statement the court accepted as truthful. The plaintiff has no procedural mechanism to test the veracity of an officer of the court's representation to the judge in real time. The only mechanism is a separate action, brought after the fact, alleging and proving that the judgment was procured by fraud that deprived the aggrieved party of a fair adversary hearing.

The equity characterization also matters because it defines the prejudice standard. In an ordinary appeal, the reviewing court applies *People v. Watson* (1956) 46 Cal.2d 818, 836 (state law: "reasonably probable" a more favorable result) or *Chapman v. California* (1967) 386 U.S. 18, 24 (constitutional: "beyond a reasonable doubt" that error was harmless). In an equity action for extrinsic fraud, those frameworks do not apply because the fraud itself is the structural defect. When the exclusion of evidence was procured by an officer of the court's intentional deceit, the reviewing court does not calculate the mathematical prejudice of the excluded evidence. The process was corrupted. The question is not "would the jury have reached a different verdict?" The question is "was the plaintiff deprived of a fair opportunity to present his case?" Under *Smith v. Superior Court* (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042, when attorney misconduct is "sufficiently egregious," it constitutes a denial of due process that renders the trial "fundamentally unfair regardless of the strength of the evidence." The fraud here is sufficiently egregious: it involved written declarations under penalty of perjury containing false factual predicates, oral representations in open court directly contradicting sworn testimony given minutes earlier, a continuing failure to correct those representations over a period of months, and a casual admission that proved the prior statements were knowing lies -- all to suppress a party admission in which the defendant told a 911 operator that he struck the pedestrian.

The 9-3 verdict underscores the structural prejudice. Nine to three is the minimum civil majority under California law. A single juror changing sides would have produced an 8-4 split -- a mistrial, not a defense verdict. The suppressed evidence includes the defendant's own words to the 911 operator ("BOOM! I just hit him. HIT HIM. There was barely anything"), an eyewitness captured on Officer Thompson's body-worn camera stating "The man in the van hit the guy" and "That man in the van hit the guy who was laying on the ground" (RT 10, p. 39, ll. 21-23; RT 10, p. 59, ll. 3-4), and CAD records corroborating the emergency dispatch timeline. It is not speculative to say that a single juror would have been persuaded by the defendant's own admission that he struck the plaintiff. The defendant authenticated that recording under oath at trial: "Yes. That is my voice." (RT 8, l. 1979.) The jury heard the authentication but was prevented from hearing the recording in a timely, orderly manner because the defense had spent months telling the court the recording was of unknown provenance and had never been produced in discovery.

The institutional reliance problem also has a temporal dimension that deepens the equity claim. The false representations did not merely affect a single ruling. They created a framework that persisted across the entire trial. Once the court accepted on February 18 that the defense was genuinely ignorant of the 911 evidence, every subsequent ruling was filtered through that premise. The continuance denial on February 18 meant Plaintiff had to proceed to trial without resolving the foundation question. The MIL grants meant the evidence started trial in an excluded posture, requiring Plaintiff to spend trial time relitigating admissibility rather than presenting his case. The supplemental report exclusion on April 16 removed corroborative evidence. The 911 deferral on April 16 delayed the recording's presentation in a way that disrupted Plaintiff's trial narrative. Each ruling compounded the last. By the time the jury deliberated, the evidentiary landscape was the product of a cascade of rulings that traced back to a single false statement: "news to me."

This cascading quality is what distinguishes the fraud from an isolated evidentiary dispute. A single erroneous ruling can be reviewed for harmless error. A pattern of rulings, all flowing from a common false premise planted by an officer of the court, is structural corruption. It cannot be unwound ruling by ruling because the rulings are interdependent. The continuance denial affected the MIL posture, which affected the foundation burden, which affected the supplemental report exclusion, which affected the 911 deferral, which affected the jury's access to the evidence. Remove the initial false premise and the entire cascade collapses. That is why equity must intervene at the root -- the judgment itself -- rather than attempting to correct individual branches of the cascade on appeal.

The defense will argue that Plaintiff should have independently subpoenaed the records, that Plaintiff had the materials since August 2024 and failed to use them, and that any problem was Plaintiff's own fault. This argument is the defense's own creation. It is the very narrative that Navaratnasingham constructed when she told the court that Plaintiff "had had the materials" and the defense was "not blocking" admission. The law does not require a litigant to assume that opposing counsel is lying to the judge. A party is entitled to rely on the candor of officers of the court. (*Blanton v. Womancare, Inc.* (1985) 38 Cal.3d 396, 403.) When the court accepted the defense's representations and structured its rulings around them, the court created a procedural landscape that Plaintiff could not escape by independent action. Plaintiff did attempt to present the evidence. The defense's false representations blocked or delayed that presentation at every turn. The equitable wrong is not the plaintiff's litigation conduct but the corruption of the process itself.

Finally, the equity characterization encompasses the full scope of the wrong, including the role of CSAA Insurance Exchange as the principal that funded, directed, and benefited from the fraud. CSAA selected and retained PSA. CSAA funded the defense, including the motions in limine and the trial representation. The MILs were filed on behalf of CSAA's insured. The February 1, 2024 subpoena was issued by CSAA's retained counsel. The verdict that the fraud produced benefited CSAA directly by extinguishing its insured's liability. Under *Howard v. American National Fire Insurance Co.* (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 498, 527-528, an insurer that selects and retains counsel is vicariously liable for the conduct of that counsel in the defense of the action. Under Civil Code section 2338 and *Anderson v. Thacher* (1946) 76 Cal.App.2d 50, 72, a principal is liable for the fraudulent representations of its agent made within the scope of the agent's authority. CSAA cannot distance itself from a fraud it paid for and from which it directly benefited. The equity action reaches not only the attorneys who made the false representations but the principal that funded and benefited from the architecture of deceit. That comprehensive reach -- from the individual attorneys to the institutional insurer -- is available only in equity, and it is one of the reasons the independent action in equity is the correct and necessary vehicle for redressing this wrong.

### VII. DAMAGES: ANCILLARY EQUITABLE MONETARY RELIEF AND THE SCOPE OF HARM

The fraud on the court did not merely produce an unjust verdict. It imposed concrete, continuing, and compounding harm on Plaintiff that extends well beyond the adverse judgment itself. This section identifies the categories of damages that Plaintiff intends to pursue as ancillary equitable monetary relief incident to vacatur in the independent action in equity (CGC-25-631801), with broader damages theories preserved in the related insurance fraud action (CGC-25-631802) and at retrial following vacatur.

### A. The Fraud Period: September 2024 Through the Present

The damages are temporally bounded to what is designated as the "fraud period." That period begins no later than September 2024, when the defense's suppression posture was already operational. On September 9, 2024, CAD identifiers were provided to Alberto Reyna -- linking identifiers for the 911, dispatch, and police incident materials that the defense would later claim were of "unknown provenance." By that date, the defense had possessed the defense file for over sixteen months following the May 4, 2023 Substitution of Attorney, had requested "all documents, records stored in any format" regarding the collision on February 26, 2024, and was actively managing the evidence in anticipation of trial. The September 2024 date also coincides with the Court granting the defense's ex parte continuance on September 17, 2024, moving the trial to January 27, 2025 -- a continuance the defense obtained even as it maintained the suppression posture that would crystalize into the false MIL declarations filed on January 17, 2025.

The fraud period runs from September 2024 through the present and encompasses: the filing of MIL Nos. 6 and 17 with their false factual predicates (January 17, 2025); the February 18, 2025 pretrial hearing at which the Court relied on the "news to me" representation; the trial in April 2025 at which additional false representations were made; the entry of judgment on April 25, 2025; and the entire post-judgment period during which Plaintiff has been forced to investigate, reconstruct, and litigate the fraud. As of March 2026, the fraud period has extended approximately eighteen months and continues to accrue.

### B. Out-of-Pocket Record, Evidence, and Process Costs

Plaintiff has incurred substantial out-of-pocket costs directly caused by the fraud on the court. These include, but are not limited to: reporter's transcripts, clerk's transcripts, certified copies, copying and printing, exhibit creation, audio and video transcription, authentication costs, storage media, postage and courier expenses, filing fees, service fees, and travel, parking, and mileage for hearings and court appearances tied to the fraud dispute. Plaintiff will present itemized records, receipts, and invoices to substantiate each expenditure. Every dollar in this category traces to the fraud: none of these costs would have been incurred if defense counsel had acknowledged receipt of the materials rather than constructing a false narrative of non-receipt and unknown provenance.

### C. Lost Earnings and Loss of Business Opportunities

The fraud on the court has consumed an extraordinary and continuing amount of Plaintiff's productive time from September 2024 through the present. Prior to the fraud period, Plaintiff's professional earning capacity was approximately $30,000 per month. Since the fraud period began, Plaintiff has been forced to divert substantial portions of working time to emergency foundation work, repeated motions and appearances necessitated solely by the false non-receipt premise, transcript acquisition and analysis, record reconstruction, preparation and prosecution of the independent equity action and related proceedings, and the ongoing management of a multi-front litigation that exists solely because defense counsel made false representations to the Court.

This diversion has resulted in lost salary, lost business opportunities, and diminished professional capacity throughout the fraud period. Plaintiff will present competent evidence and records -- including earnings history, business activity documentation, and evidence of the causal connection between the fraud-driven litigation burden and the loss of productive capacity -- to substantiate these losses. The lost earnings are segregated from ordinary litigation time; they represent only the incremental time and income loss imposed by the fraud itself. Plaintiff also intends to present evidence of specific business opportunities that were lost or foregone during the fraud period as a direct consequence of the time, attention, and resources consumed by addressing the fraud on the court.

At a documented pre-fraud earning rate of approximately $30,000 per month, the lost earnings component alone is substantial, and it continues to accrue with each month that the fraudulently procured judgment remains in place and the equity action remains unresolved.

### D. Medical, Therapy, and Pharmacy Expenses

The fraud-driven litigation strain has caused and exacerbated documented physical and medical conditions during the fraud period. Plaintiff has incurred incremental medical, therapy, and pharmacy expenses -- including therapy visits, medications, copays, and related care -- that are temporally tied to the fraud period and causally connected to the litigation strain imposed by the fraud, as distinguished from the underlying collision. Plaintiff will present treatment records, billing statements, pharmacy records, and other competent evidence to substantiate these expenses and their causal nexus to the fraud-driven period. ### E. Emotional Distress, Psychological Harm, and Physical Suffering

The fraud on the court has caused Plaintiff significant emotional distress, psychological harm, and physical suffering throughout the fraud period. The experience of being forced to litigate through a corrupted process -- watching the Court rely on representations that the certified record proves were false, being accused of late disclosure and fabrication when it was the defense that had suppressed the evidence, enduring a trial on a distorted record where the defendant's own 911 admission was procedurally obstructed, and then being forced to reconstruct the entire chain of fraud after judgment -- has imposed a severe and continuing emotional, psychological, and physical toll.

To the extent the Court permits recovery of emotional distress and psychological damages as ancillary equitable monetary relief incident to vacatur, or to the extent such damages are recoverable under applicable law in connection with fraud on the court, Plaintiff intends to seek such recovery. California courts have recognized that when attorney misconduct is "sufficiently egregious," it may constitute a denial of due process rendering the trial "fundamentally unfair regardless of the strength of the evidence." The emotional and psychological toll of enduring such a fundamentally unfair process is a concrete, documentable harm that supports equitable intervention. ### H. Reservation of Additional Damages in Related Proceedings

The damages sought in the equity action (CGC-25-631801) are deliberately limited to ancillary equitable monetary relief incident to vacatur. The broader damages architecture is preserved in related proceedings:

- **Punitive damages** against CSAA Insurance Exchange under Civil Code section 3294, sought in CGC-25-631802.

This compartmentalization is a deliberate litigation design. The equity action seeks process correction. The insurance fraud action seeks institutional accountability. Together, the three proceedings constitute a unified damages architecture in which the equity action is the keystone: without vacatur, the judgment stands and the personal injury claim remains extinguished; with vacatur, the entire damages universe reopens.
