## Abstract

On February 4, 2021, a vehicle struck Franciscus Dylan Rosario in a San Francisco crosswalk. The driver called 911 and said five words: *"I barely hit him with anything."* Those five words would become the most consequential evidence in a four-year legal war. Twenty-one days later, CSAA Insurance Exchange mailed a denial letter claiming it had "concluded" its investigation. It had not obtained the 911 recording. It had not obtained body-worn camera footage. It had not obtained dispatch records. That was the first lie.

Four years later, at the threshold of trial, defense counsel told the court the evidence was "news to me" and had "not been produced in discovery." The materials had been in the defense file for nearly two years. That was the second lie. When defense counsel casually admitted on Trial Day 7 that the body-worn camera footage was "provided by Dolan," the artifice collapsed. The transcript captured both the lie and the confession. This is the story of how one brain-injured plaintiff, proceeding alone against a multi-billion-dollar insurer and its retained law firms, built from the defense's own record an architecture of accountability that turns every defense posture into a concession.
