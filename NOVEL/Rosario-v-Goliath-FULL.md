# PROLOGUE: Five Words on a 911 Call

The voice on the recording is calm. A man standing at a San Francisco intersection, the afternoon of February 4, 2021, speaks into his phone while emergency vehicles approach. The dispatcher asks what happened. The man answers. Five words. "I barely hit him with anything."

Those five words would become the most consequential piece of evidence in a four-year legal war. They would be suppressed, contested, deferred, and fragmented. They would be denied by lawyers who had them in their files. They would be authenticated by the man who spoke them, under oath, in a courtroom where his own attorneys had spent months claiming they did not exist. They would define the collision between a brain-injured plaintiff and a multi-billion-dollar insurance company that had decided, twenty-one days after the impact, that its investigation was complete and its insured was not liable. They would expose a fraud so brazen that it would require three separate legal proceedings to unravel it. And they would remain, preserved in digital form, speaking across years, demanding to be heard.

This is the story of how that happened.

---

### I. The Call and the First Lie

Imagine the scene. A crosswalk in San Francisco. February 4, 2021. A pedestrian, Franciscus Dylan Rosario, walking his scooter through the intersection. A vehicle turning. The physics of impact. The body on the pavement. The driver, Subhi Abdelhalim, reaching for his phone. The 911 operator answering. And then the five words: "I barely hit him with anything."

The recording was captured by the San Francisco Police Department dispatch system. Under California law, such recordings are public records, obtainable through routine requests. The Dolan Law Firm, representing Rosario, obtained the 911 audio, body-worn camera footage from five responding officers, and computer-aided dispatch logs within three months. All of it routine. The kind of evidence any competent automobile liability investigator would gather before reaching a conclusion about fault.

CSAA Insurance Exchange did not. Twenty-one days after the collision, it mailed Rosario a denial letter stating it had "concluded" its investigation and determined its insured was not liable. The [CSAA Investigation Report](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/04-EXHIBITS/EXHIBIT-001-CSAA-Investigation-Report.pdf) is part of the public record. CSAA had not obtained the 911 recording, body-worn camera footage, or CAD records. Its claim file would have shown no record of those requests. The "concluded" representation was false when made. It was the first lie. As scholars of insurance industry practice have documented, the early denial is coercive: it shifts the burden of evidence gathering onto the claimant and forces litigation as the only means of obtaining what the insurer should have gathered before issuing the denial.

---

### II. The Law: Party Admissions and the Duty to Instruct

Under California Evidence Code section 1220, a party admission is not hearsay. When a defendant makes a statement offered against him, it is admissible as of right. No foundation beyond authentication is required. The defendant's own words, at the moment closest to the event, constitute the single most powerful category of evidence in civil litigation. Trial lawyers know this. The defense team knew this. Which is why the 911 recording had to be managed carefully.

At trial, Abdelhalim would testify that "no contact" occurred. His own 911 call would stand as a direct, authenticated contradiction. When asked whether the voice on the recording was his, he would answer: "Yes. That is my voice." The [Trial Day 8 transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/REPORTER-TRANSCRIPTS/Trial-Day-08-April-17-2025.pdf) preserves it at line 1979. But by then the damage was done. The jury had already heard his "no contact" testimony. The 911 recording arrived late, stripped of context. The suppression had worked.

California Vehicle Code sections 22107 and 21804(a) define the duty of care for a driver turning through a crosswalk. During deliberations, the jury asked whether they should consider these provisions. The court refused, telling them the statutes were "not part of the evidence" and "not permitted to consider." That ruling is the subject of Appeal No. A173827. The legal error is clear: statutes are law, not evidence. Evidence Code section 451(a) imposes mandatory judicial notice of California statutory law. Code of Civil Procedure section 614 imposes a mandatory duty to inform the jury on points of law it requests. The court had no authority to refuse. As *People v. Watson* (46 Cal.2d 818) and *Chapman v. California* (386 U.S. 18) establish, instructional error is reversible when it is reasonably probable a different result would have been reached absent the error. A single juror changing sides would have altered the nine-to-three verdict.

---

### III. The Suppression, the Confession, and the Second Lie

The jury did not hear those five words in a timely manner. Ten days before trial, the defense filed nineteen Motions in Limine. Motion in Limine No. 6 sought to exclude evidence "not produced in discovery," including the 911 call. Motion in Limine No. 17, signed by Priya Navaratnasingham and Robert K. Phillips, claimed the materials were "not produced during discovery, rather produced at the eleventh hour" and that the defendant had "no idea" where they came from. The motions were signed under penalty of perjury. They were false.

The recording had been produced to defense counsel during discovery. Jeremy Jessup of the Dolan Law Firm testified under oath at the February 18, 2025 pretrial hearing. The [hearing transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/HEARINGS/All-Hearing-Transcripts-Rosario-AAA.pdf) at pages 139-140 captures his words: the materials had been "produced to defense counsel" and "served on them in the production for discovery," and "all of those items were served on them... utilized in the deposition of defendants." His testimony was uncontradicted. Navaratnasingham responded: "So this is news to me... our office did not receive the 911 call until the day of the MSC." The court said: "I see. Okay."

On April 16, 2025, during the seventh day of trial, the court asked Navaratnasingham about the body-worn camera footage on the defense exhibit list. She responded: "Sure. They were provided by Dolan. He has had them for whatever, sure." The [Trial Day 7 transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/REPORTER-TRANSCRIPTS/Trial-Day-07-April-16-2025.pdf) preserves it at RT 7, page 214, lines 13-15. If the body cams were "provided by Dolan," and all three categories were produced together, the defense had the 911 recording too. "News to me" was a lie. Every exclusionary ruling that flowed from those representations was the product of fraud on the court.

On February 1, 2024, defense counsel Alberto Reyna had issued a Compex subpoena to the SFPD specifically requesting the 911 call and body-worn camera footage by name. The [subpoena](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/04-EXHIBITS/2024-02-01-Compex-Subpoena.pdf) is in the record. Fourteen months later, when Rosario attempted to play the 911 recording, Reyna objected: "I am not sure what recording he is referring to." The same attorney. The same recording. The feigned ignorance is documented.

---

### IV. The Verdict and the Architecture

The motion to continue trial was denied. The court stated that Rosario had had the materials "since August of 2024" and that the defense was "not blocking" him. Every element of that reasoning was manufactured by the fraud. The defense had had the materials since 2022. The harm was done before a single witness was sworn.

The jury returned a verdict of nine to three in favor of the defense. Nine to three is the minimum civil majority under Code of Civil Procedure section 618. The verdict was rendered by a jury that never heard the 911 party admission in a timely manner, never saw the body-worn camera footage presented alongside it, and was expressly prohibited from considering the governing statutory duty of care it had requested. The fraud had shaped everything.

The narrative is driven by two lies, each demanding the other. The first: the insurer declared its investigation complete before performing the foundational steps. The second: at the threshold of trial, successor defense counsel represented that the materials were of "unknown provenance" and "news to me." If the defense had acknowledged possession, the natural question would follow: how could the insurer have "concluded" a proper investigation twenty-one days after the collision? The first lie required the second. The second protected the first. The defense's doubling down created the contradiction that the plaintiff exploited. As *Kulchar v. Kulchar* (1 Cal.3d 467, 471) and *Aldrich v. San Fernando Valley Lumber Co.* (170 Cal.App.3d 725, 735) hold, extrinsic fraud on the court can warrant vacatur of a judgment through an independent action in equity. The equity action in CGC-25-631801 rests on that doctrine.

---

### V. The Promise

The digital file of the 911 call exists. The voice is preserved. The words are in the record. An appeal is pending before the First Appellate District, Division Three. An independent action in equity in CGC-25-631801 seeks to set aside the judgment for extrinsic fraud. A fraud and deceit action in CGC-25-631802 targets the denial letter. Three proceedings, each with its own legal theory, each drawing proof from the same immutable record. The appeal stands on instructional error alone. The equity action stands on the second lie alone. The fraud action stands on the first lie alone. Together, they create a closed logical web. Every branch of the logical tree leads to liability.

This is not a dispute about whether the defendant struck the plaintiff. The defendant said he did. "I barely hit him with anything." He authenticated those words on the stand. This is a dispute about whether the truth was permitted to reach the jury. Rosario is a senior AI systems architect. Before the collision, his W-2 compensation ranged between $550,000 and $1,000,000 per year. The traumatic brain injury he sustained did not make him less intelligent; it made his intelligence harder to deploy. When his lawyers withdrew, he continued the litigation himself, in pro per. He saw the contradiction in the record. He built the architecture that turned the defense's own words into an inescapable trap. That is the human story underneath the legal architecture.

The five words are the anchor. The transcript captured the lie and the confession in the same proceeding, from the same speaker. It is the record that will determine whether justice is done or whether the fraud stands. The record speaks for itself.

# CHAPTER 1: February 4, 2021 — The Crosswalk

On the afternoon of February 4, 2021, Franciscus Dylan Rosario was walking his scooter through a crosswalk in San Francisco when a vehicle driven by Subhi Abdelhalim struck him. The impact was not a glancing touch. It was sufficient to cause catastrophic traumatic brain injury. Rosario went from being a senior AI systems architect earning between $550,000 and $1,000,000 per year to a man whose cognitive capacities had been permanently altered. The collision would set in motion a chain of events that would expose institutional fraud, courtroom deception, and the structural vulnerabilities of the American civil justice system when a brain-injured plaintiff faces a multi-billion-dollar insurer and its retained law firms. But in that moment, in the crosswalk, there was only the impact, the body on the pavement, and the driver reaching for his phone.

---

### I. The Scene

San Francisco on a February afternoon carries a particular quality of light: grey-white coastal haze, the kind that blurs edges and softens shadows. February 4, 2021 was a Thursday. The city was still deep in the pandemic era, streets emptier than usual, but not empty. Rosario was on foot, walking his scooter through the intersection. The crosswalk is one of the fundamental compact spaces of urban life: a place where the law explicitly grants priority to the pedestrian. California Vehicle Code section 21950(a) establishes that the driver of a vehicle shall yield the right-of-way to a pedestrian crossing within any marked crosswalk. Vehicle Code section 22107 provides that no person shall turn a vehicle from a direct course or move right or left upon a roadway until such movement can be made with reasonable safety. These statutes define the duty of care for a driver turning through a crosswalk. Under Evidence Code section 669, violation of a statute that establishes a standard of conduct may give rise to a presumption of negligence. The jury would eventually ask whether they should consider these provisions. The court would refuse to answer.

Abdelhalim was turning. The details of the turn, its angle, its speed, the sight lines, the visibility of Rosario in the crosswalk, are documented in the police report, the CAD logs, and the body-worn camera footage that five SFPD officers captured at the scene. The CAD logs provided the timestamped chain connecting the 911 call to the dispatch to the arrival of each unit. Standard documentation for any traffic collision. Routine artifacts of urban infrastructure. Any insurance adjuster with basic training knows to request them.

The impact carried sufficient force to cause catastrophic traumatic brain injury. The human brain is suspended in cerebrospinal fluid within the skull, held in equilibrium by the surrounding cranial architecture. When the head undergoes a sudden acceleration-deceleration event, the brain moves within the skull cavity, its neural tissue shearing and stretching against the rigid interior surfaces. The result is diffuse axonal injury: microscopic tears in the neural pathways that coordinate the brain's various functions. These tears do not always show on standard imaging. They manifest in cognitive impairment, executive dysfunction, memory disruption, sensory sensitivity, mood dysregulation, and fatigue that extends well beyond anything a neurologically intact person would recognize as tiredness. Catastrophic TBI is not a single injury. It is a permanent alteration of the organ through which the person experiences the world.

Rosario was that person. Before the collision, he was a senior AI systems architect. His professional identity was built on the capacity to hold complex systems in mind simultaneously, to reason about interactions between components, to design architectures that were elegant, scalable, and resilient. The work required sustained cognitive effort at a high level of abstraction. It required the ability to switch between macro-level system design and micro-level implementation details, to track dependencies across months-long projects, to communicate technical complexity to non-technical stakeholders, and to maintain composure under the pressure that characterizes high-stakes technology environments. His W-2 compensation, documented in the [demand letter](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/README.md) and associated pleadings, ranged between $550,000 and $1,000,000 per year. He had built that career from nothing, through a combination of technical brilliance and relentless application.

The collision changed all of that. Not by making him less intelligent. But by making his intelligence harder to deploy.

---

### II. The 911 Call and the Chain of Evidence

Abdelhalim called 911 from the scene. The call was captured by the SFPD dispatch system, preserved as a public record. It contains five words that would become the most consequential evidence in the case: "I barely hit him with anything."

The word "barely" is doing significant work. It is not "I did not hit him." It is not "there was no contact." It is an acknowledgment of contact, qualified by an assessment of severity. The defendant, at the moment of maximum temporal proximity to the event, admitted that contact had occurred. He attempted to minimize it. Under Evidence Code section 1220, that statement is a party admission: admissible against the declarant, not hearsay, requiring no foundation beyond authentication.

The Dolan Law Firm obtained the 911 recording, five body-worn camera videos, and the CAD log through routine public records requests. Jeremy Jessup would later testify at the February 18, 2025 pretrial hearing that the firm had produced all three categories to defense counsel during discovery. The [hearing transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/HEARINGS/All-Hearing-Transcripts-Rosario-AAA.pdf) at pages 139-140 preserves his words. The production was unified; the materials were used in the deposition of the defendant. By the end of 2022, both sides had everything. The [Second Amended Complaint](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/02-CASE-CGC-25-631801/01-Second-Amended-Complaint.pdf), [Abdelhalim deposition](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/DEPOSITIONS/05-Subhi-Abdelhalim-Deposition-2022-09-30.pdf) (September 30, 2022), and [Rosario deposition](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/DEPOSITIONS/01-Franciscus-Rosario-Deposition-2022-08-18.pdf) (August 18, 2022) document the timeline. What happened next is the story of how the defense took that shared knowledge and transformed it into a weapon.

---

### III. Rosario Before: The Man the Collision Took

To understand what this case is about, it is necessary to understand who Franciscus Dylan Rosario was before February 4, 2021. The demand letter and associated pleadings describe him as a senior AI systems architect with W-2 compensation ranging between $550,000 and $1,000,000 per year over the period immediately preceding the collision. The demand letter documents past lost earnings over six years at a median rate of $750,000 per year, totaling $4,500,000. It documents future earning capacity loss, given total occupational disability, projecting at $10,000,000 to $15,000,000. It documents lifetime medical specials for catastrophic traumatic brain injury projecting at $3,000,000 to $5,000,000.

These numbers represent the economic dimension of the harm. They can be computed, discounted, actuarially projected, and argued about in expert testimony. But they do not capture what was taken. What was taken was the particular quality of mind that Rosario had spent a lifetime building: the architectural intelligence, the systems-level reasoning, the capacity to hold complexity in working memory, the executive function that allowed him to deploy those capacities reliably and sustainably under pressure. These are not abstract skills. They are the specific cognitive instruments of a specific profession that he had mastered. The collision damaged those instruments at the neural level.

The son of immigrants, Rosario built his career without institutional connections, without the networking advantages of elite educational pedigrees, without the inherited social capital that smooths the path for those born into professional-class families. He built it through technical mastery and the relentless pursuit of craft. That is not a romantic narrative. It is the factual foundation for understanding why the denial letter mattered so much. When CSAA declared its investigation "concluded" twenty-one days after the collision, it was not just denying a liability claim. It was telling a man who had just suffered a catastrophic injury, who was suddenly without the cognitive tools his livelihood depended on, who was facing years of litigation without any guarantee of outcome, that the institution responsible for compensating the person who harmed him had already decided it bore no responsibility. The denial was coercive in the specific context of this specific person's circumstances.

He is also the kind of person the American civil justice system was designed for. The system does not exist to provide additional legal resources to the already-powerful. It exists to provide a level field to those who have been wronged and who lack the institutional power to compel accountability through any other channel. When a brain-injured pedestrian is struck by a vehicle while crossing in a crosswalk, and the driver's insurer denies liability without obtaining the most basic evidence, the civil justice system is supposed to correct that outcome. The system is supposed to be the mechanism through which the driver's authenticated admission, "I barely hit him with anything," reaches the jury and the jury is permitted to evaluate it in light of the governing statutory duty of care. The question this case poses is whether the system performed that function or whether fraud prevented it from doing so.

---

### IV. The Traumatic Brain Injury as Lived Reality

A traumatic brain injury is not a metaphor for hardship. It is a specific, documented, physiological alteration of the organ through which the injured person processes every dimension of lived experience. When the medical records describe catastrophic TBI, they are describing a set of cascading consequences that extend far beyond any single symptom.

Executive function is the most professionally significant casualty. Executive function encompasses the cognitive processes that allow a person to plan multi-step tasks, monitor their own performance, switch between competing demands, inhibit irrelevant responses, and sustain goal-directed behavior over extended periods. For a senior AI systems architect, executive function is the engine of professional capability. Every project requires sustained planning. Every architecture decision requires the evaluation of competing approaches against multiple criteria simultaneously. Every client communication requires the suppression of technical details that would obscure rather than illuminate. These are not peripheral skills. They are the core of the work. The TBI degraded them.

The degradation did not eliminate them. This is the cruelest dimension of a TBI that falls short of incapacitation. Rosario could still think. He could still analyze. He could still write with the precision and syntactic discipline of someone who had spent decades working in highly technical domains. He could still construct legal arguments with the rigor of a trained lawyer. He could still see patterns in data and draw logical inferences from evidence. But the overhead cost of every cognitive act had increased dramatically. Tasks that had once been automatic required conscious effort. Sequences that had once been fluid required deliberate planning. Sustained focus, which had once been a natural state, now required active maintenance and was subject to sudden collapse. The working memory capacity that had allowed him to hold an entire system architecture in mind simultaneously was now constrained and unreliable.

Managing a multi-year, multi-front litigation against institutional opponents with unlimited resources, without the support of counsel, while simultaneously dealing with the physical, cognitive, and emotional consequences of a catastrophic brain injury: this is the context in which Rosario built the legal architecture that will be described in the chapters that follow. Every filing, every research task, every strategic decision was made under conditions of cognitive overhead that would have been debilitating for most people. He overcame those conditions through systematic method, technological tools, and the same refusal to accept unverified premises that had defined his professional career.

The [demand letter](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/README.md) documents the direct connection between the defendant's negligence and the harm: the collision caused the TBI; the TBI caused the occupational disability; the occupational disability eliminated the earning capacity; the denial letter forced the litigation; the litigation imposed its own separate burden of costs, time, and cognitive depletion. Every element of the damages chain leads back to the crosswalk on February 4, 2021, and to the five words spoken moments afterward by the driver who was there.

---

### V. The Governing Law and the Unanswered Question

Vehicle Code section 22107 requires that no person shall turn a vehicle from a direct course until such movement can be made with reasonable safety. Section 21804(a) requires yielding when entering a highway from private property or an alley. These statutes establish the governing duty of care for a driver turning through a crosswalk. They are the provisions Rosario requested in proposed CACI instruction 707, which tracks the statutory standard for negligent turning. They are the provisions the jury asked about during deliberations.

The jury's question, documented in the [Clerk's Transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/CLERKS-TRANSCRIPT/CGC-21-594102-A173827-Clerks-Transcript-Full.pdf) and at RT 11, page 7, lines 23-27, reads: "In considering the question of whether the defendant was negligent, should we consider the provisions of Vehicle Code Sections 22107 and 21804(A)?" The court refused. It told them the statutes were "not part of the evidence" and "not permitted to consider that as evidence in the case, period."

That refusal is on appeal in A173827. Code of Civil Procedure section 614 imposes a mandatory duty: when a deliberating jury "desires to be informed on any point of law arising in the case," the information "required must be given." The court had no authority to refuse. Evidence Code section 451(a) imposes mandatory judicial notice of California statutory law; statutes are law, not evidence. The error is reviewed de novo. Under *Soule v. General Motors Corp.* (1994) 8 Cal.4th 548, 580, instructional error is reversible when it is reasonably probable a different result would have been reached. Under *People v. Watson* (1956) 46 Cal.2d 818, 836, the same standard applies to state law claims. A jury that returned a nine-to-three verdict was denied the primary analytical tool for the primary question before it. One juror's mind, changed by the statutory framework or the 911 recording in full context, would have altered the outcome.

### VI. The Road That Led Here

The crosswalk on February 4, 2021 is the beginning of a story that would take five years to reach its current moment. It is the beginning because it is where the harm was inflicted: the physical impact, the catastrophic injury, the permanent alteration of Rosario's capacity to live the life he had built. But the story is not primarily about the impact. The impact lasted a fraction of a second. The consequences have lasted five years and continue. The story is about what came after. About the institutional response to the harm. About the lies that were constructed to protect the institution's financial interest. About the courtroom fraud that corrupted the proceeding in which the truth should have been heard. He could not have anticipated any of that. He simply crossed a street in San Francisco and was struck by a vehicle. The collision is the fixed point. Everything else follows from it. <a id="chapter-2"></a>
# CHAPTER 2: Twenty-One Days — The Denial Letter

Twenty-one days after the collision, CSAA Insurance Exchange mailed Franciscus Rosario a letter. The [CSAA Investigation Report](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/04-EXHIBITS/EXHIBIT-001-CSAA-Investigation-Report.pdf) dated February 25, 2021, stated that CSAA had "concluded" its investigation and determined that its insured was not liable. The word "concluded" carries an implied factual predicate: the investigation is finished because the investigative steps have been performed. A reasonable person receiving that letter from a multi-billion-dollar institution understands "concluded investigation" to mean that the basic steps of an automobile liability inquiry have been completed. Obtaining the 911 recording. Obtaining the body-worn camera footage. Obtaining the CAD dispatch records. CSAA had done none of these things. Its claim file, had anyone examined it, would have shown no record of a 911 request, no bodycam request, no CAD request. The "concluded" representation was false when made. This is the first lie. It was institutional. It was pre-litigation. And it set the anchor from which every subsequent fraud would be measured.

---

### I. The Letter and the Word

The denial letter arrived twenty-one days after the collision. February 4, 2021 to February 25, 2021. A thorough automobile liability investigation would typically include: interviews with the insured and claimant; review of the police report; requests for 911 recordings, body-worn camera footage, and CAD logs; a canvass for witnesses. These are baseline measures. The 911 call, body cams, and CAD logs were all public records, obtainable through routine written requests. The 911 call contained the driver's own words: "I barely hit him with anything."

CSAA did not make those requests. In twenty-one days, before any of these materials had been sought, let alone reviewed, CSAA's adjuster wrote that the company had "concluded" its investigation and determined its insured was not liable. The word "concluded" is completion language. It conveys that a process has been performed and finished. The letter conveyed to Rosario that a serious institution had done its homework. None of that was true. The investigation had barely begun. The 911 recording was sitting in the SFPD's records system. CSAA had not asked.

---

### II. The Implied Factual Predicate and the Legal Standard

Civil Code sections 1709 and 1710 define fraud and deceit to include the assertion of a fact not warranted by the information of the person asserting it, and the suppression of a fact by a person who is bound to disclose it. As *Lazar v. Superior Court* (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638 holds, the elements of fraud include a false representation, knowledge of its falsity, intent to defraud, justifiable reliance, and resulting damage. The denial letter's use of "concluded" implied that the investigation was finished in the sense that the investigative steps had been performed. This implied predicate was false. CSAA had not obtained the 911 recording, body-worn camera footage, or CAD records. The claim file either contains records of those requests or it does not. If it does not, the representation was false when made.

This framing transforms the denial letter from a coverage dispute into a representational event with a fixed timestamp. A coverage dispute asks whether the insurer's liability conclusion was correct; that is a judgment call, and insurers are permitted good-faith disputes. A representational event theory asks whether the factual assertion that the investigation had been completed was true. That is a binary question. The falsity is provable from the insurer's own records.

---

### III. The Institutional Mechanics

CSAA Insurance Exchange is not a small or unsophisticated entity. It is a member of the AAA federation, one of the largest automobile insurers in California, with institutional resources that dwarf anything available to an individual claimant. Its claims-handling operation runs on standardized processes designed to evaluate, manage, and close claims efficiently. "Efficiently" in the insurance business context does not always mean "thoroughly." The institutional economics of claims handling create incentives that are not always aligned with thorough investigation.

An insurance company profits when premiums collected exceed claims paid. Every claim paid is a reduction in profit. Every claim denied is, from the company's financial perspective, a favorable outcome. The institutional machinery of claims handling is designed to evaluate claims quickly and close them at the lowest possible cost. Early denial is one of the primary tools in this machinery. A denial letter mailed within twenty-one days of a collision is not the product of a leisurely investigation. It is the product of a process designed to move claims off the active file as quickly as possible. When the claimant is an injured pedestrian who lacks the resources to mount an extended litigation campaign, the early denial is often effective at achieving its purpose. Many claimants, receiving a letter from a multi-billion-dollar institution telling them that the investigation has been "concluded" and the insured is "not liable," will simply accept that determination and pursue no further legal action. The denial is designed to exploit that reality.

The institutional economics are not speculative. They have been documented by scholars, regulators, and investigative journalists over decades. The "deny-delay-defend" characterization describes a systematic pattern: deny claims early before the claimant has counsel or evidence; delay the litigation to exhaust the claimant's resources; defend aggressively to extract a settlement below the claim's true value. The courts have recognized the doctrine of insurance bad faith precisely because the institutional power imbalance creates conditions in which unfair claims handling can occur. As *Crisci v. Security Ins. Co.* (1967) 66 Cal.2d 425 established, an insurer owes a duty of good faith and fair dealing to its insured and to third-party claimants; a refusal to settle or investigate in good faith can give rise to tort liability. *Egan v. Mutual of Omaha Ins. Co.* (1979) 24 Cal.3d 809 extended the duty to the claims-handling process itself.

CSAA's twenty-one-day denial of a catastrophic-injury pedestrian claim, without obtaining the 911 recording, without obtaining the body-worn camera footage, without obtaining the CAD records that would have told any adjuster immediately whether there was documentary evidence of what occurred, is a textbook instance of the first stage of this pattern.

---

### IV. What the Contrast Reveals

The Dolan Law Firm, representing Rosario, obtained the 911 audio, body-worn camera footage, and CAD log within three months through standard public records requests. No special access. No subpoena. CSAA had twenty-one days and vastly more resources. It had an adjuster assigned to the claim. It did not obtain any of these records before sending the "concluded" letter. The implied message is inescapable: either CSAA was institutionally incompetent in a way that a small plaintiff's firm was not, or it made a deliberate decision to close the claim without completing the investigation. The 911 call contained Abdelhalim's party admission of contact. If CSAA had obtained it before the denial, its adjuster would have had to account for the driver's own words. Those words make the "no contact" defense the company would eventually fund at trial difficult to maintain on any good-faith basis.

---

### V. The Fraud Anchor and Its Consequences

The denial letter functions as what the Journal Article in the case repository calls a "fraud anchor": a fixed timestamp, documented in the public record, against which every subsequent event is evaluated. Once the anchor is established, and established cleanly, the rest of the fraud case assembles itself from the defense's own conduct.

The anchor is this: CSAA represented that its investigation was "concluded" on February 25, 2021. The record will show, through CSAA's own claim file, whether that representation was true. If the file contains no record of a 911 request, no record of a bodycam request, no record of a CAD request, then the investigation was not "concluded" in any meaningful sense at that date. The representation was false. The falsity is provable from CSAA's own records. No argument about what the liability conclusion should have been is necessary. The question is simply whether the process represented as completed had actually been performed.

This framing has several tactical advantages. First, it defeats the "opinion" defense: "concluded" is a process word, not an outcome word. Second, it creates a fixed and documentable gap. Third, it avoids the litigation privilege. Civil Code section 47(b) protects communications made in the course of judicial proceedings. The denial letter was mailed before any litigation was filed; it is an ordinary business communication in the course of claims handling. The privilege does not apply. Fourth, it creates the temporal and causal foundation for damages. Under *Prentice v. North American Title Guaranty Corp.* (1963) 59 Cal.2d 618, 620-621, when a tort forces the victim into litigation, the costs of that litigation may be recoverable as consequential damages. If the false denial letter forced Rosario into four years of litigation to obtain evidence the insurer had falsely claimed to have already reviewed, those costs are damages caused by the fraud.

The first lie set the anchor. The second lie, which would emerge four years later in a pretrial hearing, was required by the first. If the defense had acknowledged that the 911 recording existed, had been produced in discovery, and had been in their file for nearly two years, the natural question would have followed: how could CSAA have "concluded" a proper investigation twenty-one days after the collision, before the recording even existed as a recoverable artifact? The first lie required the second lie. The second lie, when it collapsed in a single sentence on April 16, 2025, validated the first. Together, the two lies created the architecture that defines this case.

### VI. The Deny-Delay-Defend Model Applied

The [First Amended Complaint](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/03-CASE-CGC-25-631802/01-First-Amended-Complaint.pdf) in the fraud action, case number CGC-25-631802, and the [Memorandum on Denial Letter Fraud](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/03-CASE-CGC-25-631802/03-Memorandum-Denial-Letter-Fraud.pdf) articulate the representational fraud theory in legal terms. The denial letter is not pleaded as an opinion about liability. It is pleaded as a factual assertion about the completion of an investigative process, an assertion that was false when made, that was made with knowledge of its falsity or with reckless disregard for its truth, and that caused Rosario to suffer damages by forcing him into years of litigation to obtain the evidence the insurer had falsely claimed to have already reviewed.

The deny-delay-defend model, as applied to this case, operated in distinct phases. The "deny" phase was the twenty-one-day denial letter: an early, conclusory denial designed to discourage pursuit of the claim. The "delay" phase was the four years of litigation that followed, during which the defense managed the proceedings in a way that stretched Rosario's resources and time while the defense had the luxury of institutional backing. The "defend" phase was the trial itself, at which point the defense had already secured, through the second lie and the resulting exclusionary rulings, a trial environment in which the most powerful evidence of liability was fragmented and delayed. The verdict of nine to three was the intended product of all three phases working together.

Rosario refused to accept the product of this process. He ordered the certified transcript. He read it. He recognized the structure of what had happened. And he built, from the defense's own record, the litigation architecture that will be described in the remaining chapters of this book. The denial letter is where that architecture begins. It is the fixed anchor. Everything returns to it.

# CHAPTER 3: The Dolan Production — The Evidence They All Had

Within three months of the collision, the Dolan Law Firm had what CSAA had not bothered to obtain in twenty-one days. The firm obtained the 911 audio, the body-worn camera footage from five responding officers, and the computer-aided dispatch logs through routine public records requests to the San Francisco Police Department. No special access. No subpoena required. A written request, the applicable fee, and the patience to wait for the records to be processed. These materials were then served on defense counsel in a single discovery production. The materials were used in depositions. Both sides had them.

---

### I. Routine Made Extraordinary by Its Contrast

The Dolan Law Firm's public records work would be unremarkable in any other context. Plaintiff's counsel routinely request police records, 911 recordings, and body-worn camera footage. The firm's work was baseline. What makes it extraordinary is the contrast: CSAA, with vastly more resources, did not submit a public records request before issuing its denial letter. The firm without institutional resources did the work in ninety days. The institution did not do it in twenty-one days.

Jeremy Jessup testified under oath at the February 18, 2025 pretrial hearing. The [hearing transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/HEARINGS/All-Hearing-Transcripts-Rosario-AAA.pdf) at pages 139-140 preserves his words: "We have, and was produced to defense counsel, body cam footage, about five videos, as well as the 911 audio, as well as the CAD log." Prior to current defense counsel's involvement, there was another firm. "All of those items were served on them in the production for discovery as well at Mr. Rosario's deposition, in addition, utilized in the deposition of defendants." His testimony was uncontradicted. It has never been impeached.

---

### II. The Three Categories and Their Contents

The 911 recording is the most legally significant. It contains Abdelhalim's statement at the scene, before counsel or adjuster. Under Evidence Code section 1220, it is a party admission: not hearsay, admissible as of right. The body-worn camera footage captured the physical environment and any witness statements at the scene. Under Evidence Code section 1280, records of a public agency are admissible as an exception to hearsay when properly authenticated. The CAD logs documented the timestamp of the call, the dispatch, and the arrival of each unit, authenticating the 911 recording by connecting it to the documented police response.

Together, these three categories constituted a comprehensive evidentiary picture. Presented in combination, they would have been devastating to the "no contact" theory. That unified picture was never assembled.

---

### III. The Discovery Production and Its Use

The materials were produced to prior defense counsel during discovery. Code of Civil Procedure section 2031 governs discovery production; the materials were "served on them in the production for discovery" and "utilized in the deposition of defendants." When new counsel substitutes in, the file transfers. California Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 1.1 requires competent representation. Incoming counsel must review the file to understand the status of the matter and the discovery that has been conducted. An attorney who takes over active litigation and makes formal representations about the discovery record without reviewing it has breached that duty.

The [Abdelhalim deposition](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/DEPOSITIONS/05-Subhi-Abdelhalim-Deposition-2022-09-30.pdf) (September 30, 2022) and [Rosario deposition](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/DEPOSITIONS/01-Franciscus-Rosario-Deposition-2022-08-18.pdf) (August 18, 2022) predate the May 4, 2023 substitution. The Dolan production was used in those depositions. The file that transferred to PSA contained the deposition transcripts. A competent review would have identified the production.

---

### IV. The Inseparability Principle

The three categories were produced together in a single discovery response. When documents are produced together, receipt of any item establishes receipt of all. The production is a single event. The defense received it.

This becomes decisive when Navaratnasingham admitted on Trial Day 7 (RT 7, page 214, lines 13-15) that the body-worn camera footage was "provided by Dolan." The [Trial Day 7 transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/REPORTER-TRANSCRIPTS/Trial-Day-07-April-16-2025.pdf) preserves it. If the body cams were provided by Dolan, and all three categories were produced together, the defense had the 911 audio too. Receipt of one is receipt of all. The [Fatal Proof document](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/FATAL-PROOF-NAVARATNASINGHAM-DOLAN-ADMISSION-EXTRINSIC-FRAUD.md) catalogs the contradiction. Every representation claiming ignorance was false.

### V. What the Record Reveals

The defense's pretrial posture of ignorance was not a casual misunderstanding. It was a sustained, multiply-repeated set of representations: MIL No. 17 claiming the materials were "not produced during discovery, rather produced at the eleventh hour"; MIL No. 6 seeking to exclude evidence "not produced in discovery"; Navaratnasingham's February 18 "news to me"; her "our office did not receive the 911 call until the day of the MSC." What happened next is the story of how one side turned that shared knowledge into a lie, and how the lie eventually destroyed itself.

# CHAPTER 4: The Substitution — Handoff or Burial?

On May 4, 2023, the defense changed lawyers. Carbone, Smith & Koyama out. Phillips, Spallas & Angstadt in. The Substitution of Attorney, Transaction ID 69953800, documented the formal transfer of the defense file from one law firm to another. Priya Navaratnasingham and Alberto Reyna of PSA became counsel of record for Abdelhalim and, by extension, for the insurance defense that CSAA was funding. Whatever prior counsel had received during discovery traveled with the file. The Dolan production had been served on Carbone, Smith & Koyama. Jessup had testified to that under oath. The file transfer meant PSA had constructive possession of the materials for approximately twenty months before trial began in April 2025.

---

### I. The Mechanics of the Substitution

Code of Civil Procedure section 284 governs substitutions of attorney. The process requires a written substitution signed by the client and incoming counsel, filed with the court. The Substitution in this case, dated May 4, 2023 (Transaction ID 69953800), is part of the official record in CGC-21-594102. It is referenced in the [case repository](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK) and [Second Amended Complaint](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/02-CASE-CGC-25-631801/01-Second-Amended-Complaint.pdf).

When counsel substitutes in, the file transfers. The file belongs to the client. Prior counsel is obligated to cooperate. Incoming counsel receives everything: pleadings, discovery, deposition transcripts, exhibits. Everything in the prior counsel's file travels to the new firm. The substitution effects a complete transfer of the client's matter. Carbone, Smith & Koyama had received the Dolan production. When CSK substituted out in May 2023, that file transferred to PSA. The file contained the Dolan production.

The twenty-month period between May 4, 2023 and January 17, 2025 is the "black box." Twenty months during which PSA had the file. Did anyone review the Dolan production? Did anyone connect the 911 recording to the MIL filings that would claim those materials were "unknown"? The answers are either in PSA's billing records or in testimony to be taken.

---

### II. The Compex Subpoena and What It Proves

There is one document that eliminates any possibility of genuine ignorance on the part of PSA about the existence and identity of the 911 recording and the body-worn camera footage. That document is the [Compex deposition subpoena](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/04-EXHIBITS/2024-02-01-Compex-Subpoena.pdf) issued by Alberto Reyna on February 1, 2024.

The subpoena, Compex Order No. CA1147102-021, was a Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records directed to the San Francisco Police Department. It was issued approximately nine months after PSA substituted in as counsel, and approximately fourteen months before trial. The subpoena requested records from the SFPD related to the incident. Importantly, it specifically requested the 911 call and the body-worn camera footage by name. Reyna knew what records existed. He knew their names. He knew where they resided. He sought them from the SFPD through a formal subpoena process.

This document destroys the "I am not sure what recording he is referring to" objection that Reyna would make at trial fourteen months later. The same attorney, the same case, the same recording, fourteen months apart. In February 2024, Reyna issued a formal subpoena specifically requesting the 911 call by name. In April 2025, when Rosario attempted to play the 911 recording at trial, Reyna told the court he was not sure what recording the plaintiff was referring to. This is not ambiguity. It is contradiction. The subpoena is the proof that the trial-day claim of uncertainty was false.

The subpoena also proves something broader about PSA's knowledge of the evidentiary landscape. A lawyer does not issue a subpoena specifically requesting a 911 call and body-worn camera footage unless that lawyer knows those records exist, knows their general character and content, and has a reason to want them in the defense's possession through an additional channel. The subpoena was not exploratory. It was targeted. Reyna was not fishing for whatever records the SFPD might happen to have. He was specifically requesting records he knew existed because the Dolan production had already established their existence. He was attempting to obtain them through a second channel, perhaps to establish a cleaner provenance than the Dolan production, perhaps to supplement the defense's own copy of the materials.

Whatever the purpose of the subpoena, its existence is fatal to any claim that PSA was genuinely ignorant of the 911 recording. The subpoena is a public court document. It is in the case repository. It is verifiable. It bears Reyna's name. It carries the date of February 1, 2024. The contradiction between that document and Reyna's trial-day claim of ignorance is captured in the official transcript. Both documents are in the record. The contradiction speaks for itself.

---

### III. The Twenty-Month Black Box and Its Legal Implications

The period between May 4, 2023 and January 17, 2025 is approximately twenty months. PSA had the file. The file contained the Dolan production. The binary trap (examined in Chapter 16) works as follows: either PSA reviewed the file when it arrived, in which case PSA had actual knowledge for twenty months before claiming "unknown provenance"; or PSA did not review the file, in which case PSA made formal legal representations about the discovery record without performing the baseline obligation of reviewing it. California Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 1.1 requires competent representation; incoming counsel must review the file. An attorney who makes representations about the discovery record without reviewing it has either proceeded with reckless disregard or made representations known to be false.

The Compex subpoena, issued eight months after PSA substituted in, is consistent with PSA having reviewed the prior file and knowing about the Dolan production. The subpoena is evidence of knowledge, not ignorance. Fourteen months later, when Reyna claimed not to know what recording the plaintiff was referring to, the contradiction was complete.

---

### IV. The File Transfer as the Mechanism of the Second Lie

The substitution of attorney on May 4, 2023 is not merely a procedural footnote in the case history. It is the mechanism through which the second lie became possible. The second lie, the defense's claim that the 911 recording and related materials were "unknown" and had "not been produced in discovery," required that someone at PSA have the ability to make that claim with a straight face. The claim was only possible if PSA could point to some gap or ambiguity in the chain of custody that created at least a colorable argument for ignorance.

The gap that the defense attempted to exploit was the substitution itself. Prior counsel CSK had received the Dolan production. But PSA was not CSK. PSA had not directly received the materials from the Dolan firm. PSA's claim was that whatever happened at CSK was CSK's responsibility, and that PSA's own office had not received the materials until the mandatory settlement conference shortly before trial. This is the factual predicate of "news to me": not a claim that the materials did not exist or had not been produced at some point, but a claim that PSA's office specifically had not received them.

The claim is destroyed by three independent pieces of evidence. First, Jessup's testimony establishes that the materials were served on prior defense counsel in the discovery production. The file transferred from CSK to PSA. The file contained the production. Constructive receipt is established. Second, the Compex subpoena establishes that Reyna, fourteen months before trial, knew about the 911 recording and body-worn camera footage with enough specificity to request them by name from the SFPD. Third, Navaratnasingham's Trial Day 7 admission establishes that the defense team understood the body-worn camera footage to have been "provided by Dolan," which, given the unified production, means the 911 audio was provided by Dolan too.

These three pieces of evidence do not require inference or reconstruction. They are in the certified record. Together, they establish that the substitution created the opportunity for the second lie, but the second lie cannot survive contact with the record that was already there.

Ten days before trial, the defense filed nineteen motions. Two of them would reshape the case. MIL No. 6 sought to exclude any "recorded materials, photographs, or documents offered by Plaintiff that were not produced in discovery," including "audio of a 911 call purported to be related to the incident." Why would a defense team with the evidence in their file claim it was unknown? Because the first lie required the second. If the defense acknowledged possession, the question would follow: how could CSAA have "concluded" a proper investigation twenty-one days after the collision, before the recording even existed as a recoverable artifact? The second lie protected the first. The motions were granted. The exclusionary framework was in place. The stage was set for a trial in which the most powerful evidence of liability would be suppressed before a single witness was sworn.

### I. January 17, 2025: The Day the Trap Was Set

Ten days before the scheduled trial date, on January 17, 2025, Phillips, Spallas & Angstadt filed a comprehensive set of pretrial motions on behalf of Abdelhalim. Nineteen Motions in Limine, a Global Statement of Facts, and a supporting Declaration of Priya Navaratnasingham. Each of the nineteen motions incorporated Navaratnasingham's declaration by reference. The declaration thus became the evidentiary backbone for every exclusionary motion PSA filed. If the declaration was false, the motions premised on it were false. If the false premises were accepted by the court, every resulting ruling was the product of false pretenses. The motions were signed by counsel under penalty of perjury, as required under the applicable rules. They became part of the official court record.

Motions in limine shape the evidentiary landscape before witnesses are sworn. They ask the court to rule in advance on admissibility. Under Evidence Code section 352, the court may exclude evidence when its probative value is substantially outweighed by the probability that its admission will create undue prejudice, confuse the issues, or mislead the jury. When the factual predicates of a motion are false, the ruling procured is not the product of the adversary system functioning properly. It is the product of a court being deceived.

The January 17 filing date is significant. It placed the false representations on the record ten days before trial. It meant that when the February 18, 2025 pretrial hearing occurred, a month before trial, the court would already have before it the filed MIL motions asserting that the 911 recording was "not produced in discovery" and that the defendant had "no idea" where it came from. Those filed motions created a context in which Navaratnasingham's hearing-room representations would be received not as startling new claims but as consistent with what was already in the record. The court had already seen the MIL filings. The "news to me" claim at the hearing was the verbal reinforcement of a written position already established in the court's file.

---

### II. MIL No. 6 — The Architecture of the Blanket Exclusion

Motion in Limine No. 6 established the broad framework. It sought to exclude from evidence any "recorded materials, photographs, or documents offered by Plaintiff that were not produced in discovery." This is, on its face, an unobjectionable standard for evidence admission: parties should not be permitted to introduce evidence at trial that they withheld during discovery. Discovery exists precisely to prevent surprise, to allow each party to evaluate and prepare responses to the evidence the opposing party intends to use. The baseline principle is sound.

The problem is the factual predicate. The motion premised the exclusionary request on the claim that the plaintiff's materials, including specifically "audio of a 911 call purported to be related to the incident," had not been produced in discovery. That premise was false. The materials had been produced in discovery. Jessup had produced them to prior defense counsel. The production was documented. The materials had been used in depositions. They were not materials produced for the first time on the eve of trial. They were materials that had been in the case record since 2022.

The court, upon receiving MIL No. 6, was being asked to apply a legitimate legal principle to a false factual predicate. The court accepted the premise. The ruling created a blanket framework: anything the court determined had not been properly produced in discovery would be excluded. This framework, premised on the false claim about production, would be used throughout the trial to manage Rosario's attempts to introduce the evidentiary record he had built from the Dolan production. Every time he tried to offer one of these materials, the motion in limine framework would be invoked. The gatekeeping was not ad hoc. It was systematic, pre-set, and based on false representations.

---

### III. MIL No. 17 — The Surgical Strike Against the Party Admission

Motion in Limine No. 17 was targeted. Where MIL No. 6 created a blanket framework, MIL No. 17 went directly after the most dangerous piece of evidence: the 911 recording containing Abdelhalim's party admission. The motion was signed by Navaratnasingham and Robert K. Phillips. It was backed by Navaratnasingham's global declaration. And it rested on three specific factual predicates, each false, each documented in the record as false.

First false predicate: the materials were "not produced during discovery, rather produced at the eleventh hour prior to trial." This is directly contradicted by Jessup's testimony that the materials were "produced to defense counsel" and "served on them in the production for discovery" and "utilized in the deposition of defendants." ### II. The "Subpoena Shield" Objection: Anatomy of an Artifice

Navaratnasingham's objection was precisely worded. She did not say the document was inadmissible. She did not argue it lacked foundation. She did not contend it was hearsay without a qualifying exception. She said: "We subpoenaed the police records for the incident and this is not anything that we received as part of the subpoena."

This is the subpoena shield. The structure of the objection is designed to create an impression without making a direct assertion. The impression: we went to the authoritative source (SFPD), we got what there was to get, and this document was not in what we received; therefore, this document's provenance is uncertain. The direct assertion: we received this specific document through a specific channel (subpoena) and it was not there.

The problem with the subpoena shield is that it answers the wrong question by answering a different question. The relevant question for admissibility purposes is not whether the defense received the document through their subpoena. The relevant question is whether the document is what it purports to be: a record generated by the SFPD in connection with the investigation of this collision. If it is, it is admissible under Evidence Code section 1280 regardless of how or through whom it came to the court's attention. A party need not receive a document through a particular channel to authenticate it or to have it admitted. The authentication question and the admissibility question are separate from the question of who received what through which discovery mechanism.

But the court, operating in real time, heard the objection through the lens of the accumulated false representations. Navaratnasingham had represented, at the February 18 hearing, that the defense had not received certain materials. She had used the "did not receive" framing consistently throughout the pretrial period. Now she was using the "not in our subpoena" framing, which was the subpoena-specific version of the same pattern: frame possession as non-receipt to create the impression that the document is unverifiable. The court heard this in context of everything that had gone before. "It is not evidence. It is not in the report." The supplemental narrative that would have corroborated Rosario's account was excluded.

Business and Professions Code section 6068(d) states that it is the duty of an attorney "never to seek to mislead the judge or any judicial officer by an artifice or false statement of fact or law." The "subpoena shield" is an artifice in the precise statutory sense: a device constructed to mislead, to create an impression that could not survive direct examination of the underlying facts. # CHAPTER 12: The Jury's Question — "Should We Consider the Vehicle Code?"

During jury deliberations on April 23, 2025, Trial Day 11, the twelve-person jury sent a written question to the court. The question, preserved at Jury Questions, page 2, lines 12-14, and at Reporter's Transcript, Trial Day 11, page 7, lines 23-27, was direct: "In considering the question of whether the defendant was negligent, should we consider the provisions of Vehicle Code Sections 22107 and 21804(A)?"

The jury was asking for the law. Not for additional evidence. Not for a re-reading of testimony. Not for clarification of the facts. They were asking whether the statutory duties governing the defendant's conduct at the time of the collision were part of the legal framework they were required to apply in evaluating his negligence. This is precisely the kind of question that juries ask when they are taking their deliberative responsibility seriously. They had identified specific statutory provisions. They had formulated a precise legal question. They wanted to know whether those provisions were relevant to their determination.

The court's response: "You may not consider Vehicle Code Sections 22107 and or 21804 subdivision A, nor will the Court instruct the Jury on those provisions." The stated basis: the statutes were "not part of the evidence" and the jury was "not permitted to consider that as evidence in the case, period."

This is a legal category error of the most basic kind. Statutes are not evidence. They are law. Evidence is testimony, documents, physical exhibits: the material from which juries determine facts. Law is the set of rules that governs the legal significance of those facts. A jury does not "consider statutes as evidence." A jury applies law to facts. The governing question in a negligence case is whether the defendant's conduct violated the applicable standard of care. In a case where the defendant made a turning movement and struck a pedestrian in a crosswalk, the applicable statute governing turning movements (Vehicle Code section 22107) and the applicable statute governing yielding to pedestrians (Vehicle Code section 21950(a)), as well as the statute the jury specifically asked about governing drivers entering a highway from a private road (Vehicle Code section 21804(a)), are directly relevant to whether the defendant met his legal duty. To tell a jury it may not consider the statute that defines the duty is to tell the jury it may not apply the law. This is error.

The error is the subject of the pending [appeal](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/tree/main/01-APPEAL), Case No. A173827, First Appellate District, Division Three. Under de novo review, a court of appeal examines instructional error by asking whether the instruction given, or refused, was legally correct, and whether any error was prejudicial. Here the error was not a question of degree: the court committed a categorical legal error by treating statutes as evidence and refusing to inform the jury on the governing law. Code of Civil Procedure section 614 is unambiguous: when the jury "desires to be informed on any point of law," the information "required must be given." The jury asked. The court refused. The statute is mandatory.

---

### I. Deliberations: The Jury's Active Engagement

The jury had heard eleven trial days of evidence. They had identified specific Vehicle Code provisions and asked a legally precise question: should we consider these in evaluating negligence? This is the question of a jury engaging carefully with the legal framework. The jury's question was a jury-generated confirmation that the instruction was necessary.

---

### II. Vehicle Code Sections 22107 and 21804(a): The Governing Law

Vehicle Code section 22107 provides that no person shall turn a vehicle from a direct course or move right or left upon a roadway until such movement can be made with reasonable safety. The statute governs turning movements and establishes the duty that a driver owes to other users of the road when making such a movement. In a case involving a vehicle that was making a turning movement at the time it struck a pedestrian, section 22107 provides the direct statutory statement of the driver's duty. Negligence per se applies: if the defendant violated section 22107, that violation establishes negligence as a matter of law, subject only to the causation and damages determinations.

Vehicle Code section 21804(a) governs the duty to yield when entering a highway from a private road or driveway. The statute requires a driver emerging from a private road or driveway to yield the right of way to all approaching traffic that is close enough to constitute an immediate hazard. In the context of this collision, section 21804(a)'s requirement to yield is directly relevant to whether the defendant, in making the movement that led to the contact with Rosario, satisfied his legal duty.

Both statutes were specifically named in the jury's question. The jury had identified the governing legal framework on its own, without additional instruction. They were asking whether they could apply that framework. The court told them they could not. The court told them the statutes were not evidence. The court told them they could not consider the provisions.

CACI 700 provides the general negligence instruction. CACI 707, which Rosario had specifically requested, provides the instruction for negligent turning or changing lanes. Evidence Code section 451(a) requires mandatory judicial notice of the provisions of every duly enacted California statute. When the jury asks whether it should consider specific provisions of California law, the answer required by law is yes. The court's answer was the opposite of what the law requires.

---

### III. The Legal Category Error

The court's stated basis was that the statutes were "not part of the evidence." This is a legal category error. Statutes are law, not evidence. Evidence Code section 451(a) requires mandatory judicial notice of California statutory law. Code of Civil Procedure section 614 is unambiguous: when the jury "desires to be informed on any point of law," the information "required must be given." The jury asked. The court refused. Under *Soule v. General Motors Corp.* (1994) 8 Cal.4th 548, 580, instructional error is prejudicial when it is reasonably probable that a result more favorable to the appealing party would have been reached absent the error. The jury returned nine to three. The governing statutory duties, had the jury been permitted to apply them, would have provided a negligence per se framework under which the defendant's conduct could have been evaluated. For a verdict that close, the error could readily have altered the outcome.

### I. The Verdict: Nine to Three

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 618, a verdict in a civil trial may be rendered by three-fourths of the jury. In a twelve-person jury, three-fourths is nine. A verdict of nine to three is the minimum majority. It is the threshold at which a civil verdict can stand. Below nine, the jury is hung. Above nine, the margin increases. At nine exactly, the result is final but barely so: one fewer vote for the prevailing side, and the outcome is different.

The significance of this minimum majority is analytical. A verdict rendered by twelve to zero reflects a jury that was persuaded unanimously. A verdict of ten to two reflects a jury that reached a strong consensus with limited dissent. A verdict of nine to three reflects a jury that was divided, with twenty-five percent of its members unconvinced by the evidence the majority found persuasive. ### III. The Structural Argument: Extrinsic Fraud

The verdict of nine to three was not the product of a fair adversary hearing. It was the product of a proceeding in which the adversary system had been corrupted at its foundation. Not by witness perjury of the kind that juries are equipped to evaluate by weighing credibility. By attorney fraud directed at the court, manipulating the procedural and evidentiary framework within which the jury would deliberate.

Extrinsic fraud is fraud that prevents a party from having a fair adversary hearing on the merits. It is distinguished from intrinsic fraud, which is fraud within the proceeding that the adversary system is designed to expose and that the jury evaluates. Intrinsic fraud is a witness lying on the stand; the adversary process has mechanisms for that: cross-examination, impeachment, counter-evidence. Extrinsic fraud is a party manipulating the procedural framework of the proceeding so that the adversary process itself cannot function correctly. The adversary process cannot correct extrinsic fraud because the fraud operates on the structure of the process itself.

The false representations made to the court in this case are extrinsic fraud in the technical legal sense. They were not misrepresentations to the jury for the jury to evaluate. They were misrepresentations to the judge, shaping the judge's rulings on continuances, evidentiary motions, and jury instructions. The jury never knew about the false representations. The jury could not evaluate them. The jury deliberated within a procedural and evidentiary framework that had been corrupted by the fraud before the jury was ever empaneled.

Under Kulchar v. Kulchar (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467, equity will set aside a final judgment where extrinsic fraud prevented a party from having a fair adversary hearing on the merits. The equity action in CGC-25-631801 is the procedural vehicle for that remedy. The nine-to-three verdict is the evidence of prejudice: the trial produced a minimum-majority verdict in a case where, had the plaintiff been able to present the full evidentiary picture without the disruption the fraud caused, the result could have been different.

### III. The Equity Action (CGC-25-631801)

The equity action is the second front. It is an independent action to set aside the judgment in the underlying personal injury case on the grounds of extrinsic fraud. The legal theory is drawn from Kulchar v. Kulchar (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467, which holds that a court of equity may vacate a judgment where the judgment was obtained through fraud that prevented the losing party from having a fair adversary hearing on the merits.

The distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic fraud is fundamental to this theory. Intrinsic fraud is fraud within the proceeding: a witness lying on the stand, a party presenting fabricated documents, a lawyer making false arguments about the evidence. The adversary system has mechanisms for intrinsic fraud: cross-examination, counter-evidence, rebuttal. Courts do not generally vacate judgments for intrinsic fraud because the party who was defrauded had the opportunity to expose the fraud through the adversary process. Extrinsic fraud is different. It is fraud that corrupts the adversary process itself: manipulating the procedural framework, deceiving the court, preventing the other party from having a fair opportunity to present their case. Because the fraud operates on the structure of the proceeding, the adversary process cannot expose it from within. Equity intervenes from without.

The false representations in this case are extrinsic fraud. They were made to the court, not to the jury. They corrupted the procedural framework: the continuance denial, the evidentiary motions, the exclusionary rulings. They prevented Rosario from having a fair opportunity to present his case. The Kulchar doctrine requires three elements: (1) the representor had knowledge of the falsity; (2) the false representations were made to the court; (3) the court relied on the representations in making rulings that deprived the plaintiff of a fair hearing. All three elements are documented in the certified transcript.

The equity action names seven defendants: Subhi Abdelhalim, CSAA Insurance Exchange, Phillips Spallas & Angstadt LLP, Priya Navaratnasingham, Alberto Reyna, Michael R. Chambers, and Carbone Smith & Koyama. ### II. The Successor Counsel Trap

The core binary for PSA and its attorneys is the file review question. ### I. The Design Philosophy: Binary Compulsion

Binary compulsion is the design principle. For each key factual proposition, the RFA is drafted so that both the admission and the denial advance the plaintiff's theory. An admission establishes the proposition as a judicial admission, binding on the party for all purposes in the litigation. A denial that contradicts a document creates impeachment material: when the deponent later testifies, the false denial can be shown to be false by the document it contradicts, and the false denial itself becomes evidence of consciousness of guilt, knowledge of the adverse fact, and willingness to misrepresent. In either case, the plaintiff has gained something.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.250 requires responses within thirty days of service. The defendant must choose positions before full investigation, before depositions, before the full picture of plaintiff's proof. Under *Burch v. Gombos* (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 356, 364, admissions are binding judicial admissions. Early positions become commitments that constrain deposition and trial options.

The simultaneous service of RFAs with the complaint maximizes this pressure. Most defendants expect to have the time between service and the start of discovery to assess the case before being required to commit to factual positions. When RFAs arrive with the complaint, the defendant is committed to positions on the core facts before the litigation has fully developed. If those positions are later shown to be false, the false RFA responses become evidence in the fraud action, the equity action, and the bar complaints simultaneously.

# CHAPTER 20: Deny, Delay, Defend — The Insurance Industry's Playbook

The deny-delay-defend model is not a conspiracy theory. It is a business model, documented by scholars, regulators, and investigative journalists. Stage 1: Deny. The early denial as coercive weapon. From the first denial letter to the courtroom fraud. Not an aberration. The system working as designed. The human cost: the cognitive burden, the financial drain, the years lost. The denial that forced litigation. The fraud that corrupted it.

The deny-delay-defend model describes the institutional logic of how large insurers manage personal injury claims from claimants without institutional resources. It was identified and named by scholars and regulators who studied the patterns of insurer conduct over decades. It is not a description of individual bad actors. It is a description of a business model: a systematic approach to claim management that maximizes the insurer's financial outcome at the expense of legitimate claimants. The model works because the power asymmetry between institutional insurers and individual claimants makes it work. Rosario's case is a granular, record-documented instance of the model operating in exactly the way it was designed to operate.

The institutional economics reward the model. An insurer that denies early, forces litigation, and defends aggressively has lower claim payments than one that settles promptly. *Crisci v. Security Ins. Co.* (1967) 66 Cal.2d 425 and *Egan v. Mutual of Omaha Ins. Co.* (1979) 24 Cal.3d 809 establish the insurer's duty of good faith; bad faith denial exposes the insurer to extracontractual liability. The human cost is externalized onto claimants.

### I. Stage 1: Deny

The denial letter of February 25, 2021 is Stage 1. Twenty-one days after the collision. The [CSAA Investigation Report](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/04-EXHIBITS/EXHIBIT-001-CSAA-Investigation-Report.pdf) documents what the investigation actually consisted of. "Concluded" is the language of institutional certainty: we have done our work, we have reached our determination, and our determination is no.

In the context of a traumatic brain injury claim, the claim that an investigation can be "concluded" in twenty-one days is substantively implausible. Traumatic brain injuries are not fully assessable in the immediate aftermath of the injury. The brain's response to trauma unfolds over time. Symptoms can be delayed. Cognitive deficits may not be fully apparent until the injured person attempts to return to normal activities. A thorough investigation of a TBI claim requires consultation with treating physicians, review of prior medical records, assessment of the claimant's functional status, and sufficient time for the full extent of the injury to manifest. None of this can be accomplished in twenty-one days.

The early denial is not merely premature. It is coercive. A claimant who receives a denial letter from the insurer has three options: accept the denial, negotiate, or litigate. Litigation is expensive, time-consuming, and emotionally depleting. For a claimant without institutional resources, without prior litigation experience, without the legal knowledge to navigate the civil justice system, the receipt of a denial letter from a well-resourced insurer carries an implicit message: this is going to be hard. Many claimants, confronted with that message, accept the denial. The insurer saves the claim payment. The model works.

From the February 2021 denial to the April 2025 trial is four years. Four years of litigation: pleadings, discovery, depositions, motions, hearings, and ultimately trial. The financial drain of the litigation, in out-of-pocket costs and in lost productive time, accumulated over four years. The asymmetry of the delay is the model's central feature. For the institutional defendant, delay is a tactical asset: evidence becomes harder to locate over time, witnesses' memories fade, the plaintiff's resources are depleted, and the pressure to accept a discounted settlement increases. For the individual plaintiff, delay is a burden. The model exploits this asymmetry systematically.

Stage 3 in this case was not merely aggressive defense. It was fraud. The civil justice system was designed for people without inherited power or corporate backing. *Faretta v. ### I. The Foundational Obligation

Business and Professions Code section 6068(d) is one of the oldest provisions of California attorney ethics law. It imposes on every attorney the duty to "employ, for the purpose of maintaining the causes confided to him or her those means only as are consistent with truth, and never to seek to mislead the judge or any judicial officer by an artifice or false statement of fact or law." The statutory language is worth parsing carefully.

"Means only as are consistent with truth": not merely a prohibition on lying, but an affirmative requirement that the attorney's means, the tactics, the arguments, the representations, must be consistent with truth. An argument that is technically accurate but designed to create a false impression violates this standard. An objection that is technically limited to one channel of receipt but designed to create the impression of complete non-possession is an artifice within the meaning of the statute.

"Never to seek to mislead the judge... by an artifice": the prohibition on artifice is independent of the prohibition on false statements. An artifice is a device or stratagem designed to mislead without technically lying. The "subpoena shield" is an artifice: an objection framed in terms of what the defense received through its own subpoena, designed to create the impression that the materials had not been received through any channel, when in fact they had been received through the Dolan production. This is the statutory definition of the conduct the statute prohibits.

California Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 3.3(a)(1) provides that a lawyer shall not knowingly make a false statement of fact or law to a tribunal. "Knowingly" is defined in the Rules to include actual knowledge and, under some circumstances, knowledge that can be inferred from the circumstances. A lawyer who makes a representation about the discovery record in a case the lawyer has been managing for twenty months without reviewing the file has not acted "without knowledge"; the knowledge is imputed from the circumstances of the representation.

The presumption of candor is the judicial system's operating assumption that attorneys appearing before the court are telling the truth. Courts extend to attorneys the trust that their professional obligations warrant. ### III. Kulchar and the Equity Remedy

Kulchar v. Kulchar (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467 established the California rule that a court of equity may set aside a final judgment obtained through extrinsic fraud. The case involved a divorce judgment obtained through the failure to disclose material information to the court. The California Supreme Court held that equity would intervene to vacate a judgment where the prevailing party had engaged in extrinsic fraud that prevented the losing party from having a fair adversary hearing on the merits. The principle has been extended and refined in subsequent cases, but the core rule remains: extrinsic fraud is a ground for equitable vacatur of a final judgment.

The equity action in CGC-25-631801 is the procedural vehicle for that remedy. The Kulchar doctrine requires proof of three elements: (1) the representor had knowledge of the falsity of the representation; (2) the false representation was made to the court; and (3) the court relied on the representation in making rulings that deprived the other party of a fair hearing. Each element is documented in the certified transcript and the supporting documents.

Reliance and deprivation: the court's rulings following each representation document the reliance. The continuance denial at hearing transcript page 181. The evidentiary rulings at trial. Rosario was deprived of the fair adversary hearing on the merits that the proceeding should have provided.

The remedy: vacatur of the judgment and a new trial on a fair record. The duty of candor was betrayed. Equity will restore what the betrayal took.
